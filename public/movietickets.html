Overview

Since the year 2000, MovieTickets.com has been offering moviegoers a quick and easy way to purchase their tickets online before going to the movie theater. With a past history of focusing mostly on ad revenue, the company recognized that by creating a better user experience their customers will want come back to the site. Back in the early days of inception, MovieTickets.com was one of the only options to pre-purchase online movie tickets before heading to the theater. With this no longer being the case, the company recognized the importance of focusing on a positive user experience while continuing to nurture the revenue with advertisements.

Objective

Build a larger user base, increase ticket sale conversions, grow advertising CPM

  • Build a fresh, new user experience
  • Rebrand company to a fun, modern style
  • Strategically place advertisements in non-obtrusive yet effective positions
  • Bring frontend development technologies up to speed with today's standards
    • Reorganize all frontend architecture
    • Use the latest coding languages
    • Output a fully responsive layout

Establishing a Vision

Taxonomy

I wanted to create a visualization of the proposed architecture of the new website content structure. This visual helped stakeholders see how users would organically navigate through the site. I went through approximately a dozen iterations after carefully combing through the sites intended user flows.

Sketches and wireframes

This is where the real fun began. Sketches and wireframes allowed the team to dream big. Quick mockups of multiple new features would help the stakeholders to recognize which items would be a necessity for launch and which could become in later iterations. It was in this stage where the refining of features could really take place. Gathering the reactions and feedback from stakeholders also helped to know if some layouts and features were going too far off the projected redesign plan. This is when dreams would be reeled in to a more realistic expectations. For example, you will see Google Maps in some layouts of the sketches, but when meeting with stakeholders and discussing tight deadlines, the maps features were moved off the requirements list and put on the ‘nice-to-have’ list.

Style Guide

I find that creating a style guide can be the most challenging part of a redesign. Especially when the style guide is not an iteration of the current guide, but a complete do-over of the current version. Why is this part of the process so hard? Well, because we all have different tastes. And getting everyone to agree on this subject can be a challenge. But by listening to feedback and properly defending decisions, I eventually got the room to agree. And beyond that, we were all excited to see the styles come more to life!

Ad Presentation

The stakeholders for the ad sales department had a very defined objective: Sell ads and make the most money possible. Can you blame them? It was a delicate balance for me to represent the frontend and design side of the business while compromising and understanding the advertisement side of the business. They were used to calling the shots in the past. But with the recent shift to focus on the user experience, I was careful to keep their stakeholders updated weekly with progress reports and clearly define how I was considering ad revenue to still be a priority with the design decisions.

Mocks & Prototypes

Where all the planning starts to really come together. Taking the concepts and layouts from the wireframes, adding the style guide, and showing actual page mocks is when the fantasy becomes a reality! I used Invision App to put together working prototypes and allowed stakeholders to feel like they could get their hands on a working product, before I actually had to commit to code. They supplied feedback and the iteration life cycle began.

UX Testing, Iteration Cycle & Mock Finalization

With working mocks in tote, I was able to gather important user feedback so changes were made from educational guess, not just emotional responses. Much about design is emotion based, but once you gather actual data to confirm or deny your hypothesis, then you follow the facts and finalize mocks with effective results.

Frontend &
The Development
Lifecycle

Each major feature or page had its own epic and was comprised of dozens of stories. I was able to write all frontend stories for my team of developers to pick up and begin building the frontend of the website. The stories contained links to documentation where business rules were clearly stated with mocks, links to prototypes, and the style guide. I prioritized and assigned stories to frontend team members, including myself, for the appropriate tasks.

When tasks where marked as ‘done’ by frontend developers, I would do code reviews to ensure proper protocols were being followed, everything passed design standards and the story was appropriate to be moved onto the testing period.

Beyond the code reviews, I would work very closely with the QA team to aid in cross browser and multi-device testing. We were building the companies first fully responsive website so there where a lot of views to check the website on. To hit a tight target deadline, I was more than willing to help get the product delivered fast but in the best state possible.

With this efficient development lifecycle in place, the entire development team was able to release the newly redesigned website to production in a seven month period.

Architecturing the Frontend Codebase

The site redesign gave me the opportunity to architect the frontend code base into a flexible system that many team members can easily adhere to. This modular system improved team efficiency and decreased development headaches by using the SMACSS methodology in combination with Sass.
